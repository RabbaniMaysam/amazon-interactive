Prediction 1 · Empirically tested

1P listings are priced below 3P listings within a market

The model's pricing FOCs deliver $p_S = c/(1-\tau)+1$ and $p_A = c + 1 + \tau\, p_S$. Both rise in $\tau$, but $p_S$ rises faster, so $p_A < p_S$ at every interior $\tau$.

What you're seeing

The two curves are $p_A(\tau)$ and $p_S(\tau)$ over the feasible interval, computed from the model's pricing first-order conditions.

The shaded region between them is the within-market gap. A vertical line marks the calibrated equilibrium fee $\tau^*$.

Prediction 2 · Empirically tested

The within-market 1P–3P price gap widens with $\tau$

From the pricing FOCs, $p_S - p_A = \tau(c/(1-\tau)+1) - c - \tau\,p_S = c(\tau/(1-\tau) - 1)$ rises monotonically over the interior. Higher referral fees translate one-for-one (and then some) into a wider gap.

What you're seeing

$\Delta p(\tau) = p_S(\tau) - p_A(\tau)$ as a function of the referral fee. A vertical line marks the calibrated $\tau^*$.

Prediction 3 · Empirically tested

The 1P sales share rises with self-preferencing

$s_A(\sigma, \tau) = F\,e^{\sigma-\tau}/(1-\tau)$ is strictly increasing in $\sigma$ at any fixed $\tau$. Self-preferencing converts visibility into share.

What you're seeing

$s_A(\sigma)$ over the range $[0, \sigma_{\max}]$, evaluated at the calibrated fee $\tau^*$.

A marker indicates the joint-optimum $\sigma^*$.

Prediction 4 · Empirically tested

Hybrid platforms favor their own products in search rankings ($\sigma>0$)

The platform's profit $\Pi(\sigma,\tau)$ is supermodular: at any $\tau$, the interior $\sigma$-FOC has a strictly positive solution. The 3D surface below shows $\Pi(\sigma,\tau)$ with the joint optimum $(\sigma^*, \tau^*)$ marked.

What you're seeing

The platform's profit as a function of fee and self-preferencing. The interior peak is the joint optimum.

Strategic complementarity ($\Pi_{\sigma\tau}>0$) is visible in the ridge running through the optimum.

Prediction 5 · Empirically tested

Hybrid platforms charge higher fees than pure marketplaces ($\tau^* > \tau^M$)

A pure marketplace has $\sigma=0$ by construction; its equilibrium fee is $\tau^M = b_\tau(0)$. By strategic complementarity, the hybrid's joint optimum sits on a strictly higher best-response: $\tau^* = b_\tau(\sigma^*) > b_\tau(0) = \tau^M$.

What you're seeing

The platform's best-response fee $b_\tau(\sigma)$ as a function of $\sigma$. Two markers identify $\tau^M$ (at $\sigma=0$) and $\tau^*$ (at the equilibrium $\sigma^*$).

Prediction 7 · Empirically tested

Categories with higher 3P revenue ($\theta\cdot m$) are more likely to host a 1P listing

In the dynamic extension, the platform enters category $\theta$ if and only if $\theta \ge \theta^e = K(1-\delta)/m(\sigma,\tau)$. Larger categories cross this threshold; smaller ones do not.

What you're seeing

The category-size distribution $\theta \in [0,1]$, with the entry threshold $\theta^e$ marked.

Categories to the right of $\theta^e$ are platform-entered; to the left, marketplace-only.

Prediction 8 · Empirically tested

The 1P–3P gap is generated by raising 3P costs, not by below-cost 1P pricing

$dp_S/d\tau = c/(1-\tau)^2$ dominates $dp_A/d\tau = c/(1-\tau)^2 + 1$ in absolute response (since $p_S$ rises from a smaller base in percentage terms). Empirically, our MIT within-category trends confirm: 3P prices rose 2.4% per year while 1P prices were flat.

What you're seeing

The same two price curves as Pred 1, but with slope annotations highlighting the asymmetric responsiveness of $p_S$ vs $p_A$ to $\tau$ at the calibrated equilibrium.

Prediction 9 · Descriptively examined

The number of active 3P sellers ($N_S$) falls as the fee rises

From the free-entry condition, $N_S(\tau) = (1-\tau)\,s_S(\sigma^*,\tau)/F$. Both factors decline as $\tau$ rises, so $N_S$ is strictly decreasing.

What you're seeing

$N_S(\tau)$ as a function of the referral fee, evaluated at the equilibrium $\sigma^*$.

Prediction 10 · Descriptively examined

Higher consumer valuation ($v$) raises both $\tau^*$ and $\sigma^*$

Two parameter comparative statics: $d\tau^*/dv > 0$ and $d\sigma^*/dv > 0$. Higher consumer valuation makes both the fee and self-preferencing more profitable for the platform.

What you're seeing

The dual-axis curve plots $\tau^*(v)$ and $\sigma^*(v)$ over a range around the calibrated value of $v$.

Prediction 11 · Descriptively examined

The platform's footprint ($Y$) rises over time, following a transitional ratchet

The fee path satisfies $\tau^M < \tau^0 < \tau_{ss}$: the platform raises $\tau$ above the pure-marketplace level immediately upon any category entry, then ratchets further as the footprint accumulates.

What you're seeing

$Y_t$ and $\tau_t$ over the dynamic transition, with horizontal references at $\tau^M$, $\tau^0$, and $\tau_{ss}$.

Prediction 12 · Descriptively examined

Self-preferencing harms consumers primarily through its effect on the fee

Consumer welfare $W$ depends only on $\tau$, not directly on $\sigma$. Hence at fixed $\tau$, $W(\sigma)$ is flat ($W_\sigma = 0$). But the platform's best-response $\tau = b_\tau(\sigma)$ rises in $\sigma$, so along the equilibrium path $W$ falls.

What you're seeing

Two curves: $W(\sigma \mid \tau^*\text{ fixed})$ (the flat one — Pred 19) and $W(\sigma \mid \tau=b_\tau(\sigma))$ (declining).

The shaded gap between them quantifies the indirect channel: how much welfare loss is mediated by the platform's fee response.

Prediction 13 · Descriptively examined

Pure marketplaces have higher 3P-seller density than hybrid platforms

Combining Pred 5 ($\tau^M<\tau^*$) and Pred 9 ($N_S$ decreasing in $\tau$): a pure marketplace has fewer-fee, more-seller equilibrium; a hybrid has more-fee, fewer-seller.

What you're seeing

Two bars compare $N_S$ at the pure-marketplace equilibrium ($\sigma=0, \tau^M$) and the hybrid equilibrium ($\sigma^*, \tau^*$).

Prediction 14 · Future / structural

When the platform enters a category, the fringe share $s_S$ falls discretely

A category transitions from marketplace-only (no $\sigma$, fee $\tau^M$) to platform-entered ($\sigma^*, \tau_t$). At the moment of entry, 3P share drops by approximately $s_A(\sigma^*, \tau_t)$.

What you're seeing

The simulated category-level $s_S$ trajectory across an entry event: flat at the marketplace-only level, then a discrete fall as $s_A$ takes share.

Prediction 15 · Future / structural

Higher fees ($\tau$) reduce 3P investment in product innovation

The seller's innovation FOC gives $\rho'(I^*)\,\theta\,\pi^{inn}_S(\tau) = 1$. With $\pi^{inn}_S$ declining in $\tau$ (since per-consumer profit is squeezed), $I^*(\tau)$ is decreasing.

What you're seeing

A stylized $I^*(\tau)$: the seller's optimal innovation effort as a function of the platform's fee, under a CES innovation success function $\rho(I) = 1 - e^{-rI}$ with normalized parameters.

Prediction 16 · Future / structural

Capping $\tau$ lowers equilibrium $\sigma^*$, and vice versa

By strategic complementarity, the platform's best responses $b_\sigma(\tau)$ and $b_\tau(\sigma)$ are both increasing. A regulatory cap on one instrument propagates to the other through the equilibrium.

What you're seeing

Two panels. Left: $\sigma^*(\bar\tau)$ — the equilibrium self-preferencing as a function of a binding fee cap $\bar\tau$. Right: $\tau^*(\bar\sigma)$ — the equilibrium fee as a function of a binding self-preferencing cap $\bar\sigma$.

Prediction 17 · Future / structural

Long-run welfare loss has static and dynamic components

The long-run loss decomposes into a starting-tilt piece ($\tau^M \to \tau^0$, from immediate $\lambda Y^e$ entry) and a transitional ratchet piece ($\tau^0 \to \tau_{ss}$, from further footprint accumulation).

What you're seeing

A stacked bar showing the two components of the long-run consumer-welfare loss relative to the pure-marketplace benchmark.

Prediction 18 · Future / structural

Long-run footprint rises in $\delta$, falls in $K$; entry speed $\lambda$ accelerates without altering the steady state

The steady-state threshold $\theta^e_{ss} = K(1-\delta)/m$ delivers $\partial Y_{ss}/\partial \delta > 0$, $\partial Y_{ss}/\partial K < 0$, $\partial Y_{ss}/\partial \lambda = 0$. $\lambda$ affects only the transition shape.

What you're seeing

Three small panels: $Y_{ss}(\delta)$, $Y_{ss}(K)$, and overlaid transition curves $Y_t$ for three values of $\lambda$ (all converging to the same steady state).

Prediction 19 · Future / structural

The direct effect of $\sigma$ on consumer welfare, holding $\tau$ fixed, is exactly zero

$W$ depends only on $\tau$ via consumer surplus $\ln D$, and $D$ is pinned down by the 3P free-entry condition that depends only on $\tau$ (not $\sigma$). The 3P market absorbs all of $\sigma$'s effect, leaving $W_\sigma = 0$.

What you're seeing

$W(\sigma)$ with $\tau$ held fixed at the equilibrium value — a flat line. Compare to Pred 12 for the indirect channel.

Prediction 20 · Future / structural

The platform's privately optimal fee exceeds the welfare-maximizing fee: $\tau^W < \tau^*$

The platform internalizes $\Pi'(\tau) = 0$ but ignores $W'(\tau) < 0$. The welfare-maximizing fee $\tau^W$ satisfies $W'(\tau^W) + \Pi'(\tau^W) = 0$, and lies strictly below $\tau^*$.

What you're seeing

$W(\tau)$ and $\Pi(\tau)$ on dual axes, with $\tau^W$ and $\tau^*$ marked. The shaded region between them is the deadweight loss.

Prediction 21 · Future / structural

Combined regulation yields strictly higher consumer welfare than either single-instrument regime

By strategic complementarity, capping one instrument tightens the equilibrium of the other. A combined regime (fee cap and non-discrimination mandate) is strictly more welfare-improving than either alone.

What you're seeing

Four bars compare consumer welfare $W$ under: laissez-faire, fee cap only, non-discrimination only, and combined regulation.
